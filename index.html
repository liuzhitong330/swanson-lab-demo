Motivation The signature Reading it Relevance

The lab's view of Parkinson's disease centers on oxidative stress. Dopamine neurons in the substantia nigra run a demanding metabolism, and their main defense against the resulting oxidative damage is glutathione. The lab has long studied how neurons make and use glutathione, and how that defense fails, and measured glutathione in the Parkinson's substantia nigra drops to roughly 40 percent of normal.

I wanted to see what that failure looks like in real gene expression. I took a postmortem study of human substantia nigra, 9 controls and 16 Parkinson's cases, and asked how the dopamine machinery, the glutathione and antioxidant system, and iron handling change in disease.

How to read the change

Each bar is one gene's change in Parkinson's tissue compared to control, on a log2 scale, so a bar of minus one means expression roughly halved. Bars to the left are lower in disease, bars to the right are higher. Genes marked in red pass a simple significance cutoff. Because this is bulk tissue, the loss of dopamine genes partly reflects that dopamine neurons have died, while changes in the antioxidant genes reflect the surviving mixed tissue.

Analysis 2 — A targeted defect, not a collapse

The antioxidant system does not fail across the board. Most of its genes, including the superoxide dismutases, catalase, and the cystine transporter, barely move. What stands out is glutathione synthetase, GSS, the enzyme that completes glutathione production. It is the most reduced antioxidant gene in the set and the most statistically clear, alongside the secreted peroxidase GPX3. At the same time both ferritin genes rise, which fits the iron accumulation long described in the Parkinson's substantia nigra.

So the picture is specific rather than diffuse. The tissue keeps most of its antioxidant enzymes but loses a step of glutathione synthesis while loading up on iron, which is exactly the kind of imbalance the lab's glutathione and oxidative-stress work would predict, and it lines up with the measured drop in glutathione.

Why this matters for the lab

A gene-level view like this turns a broad idea, that glutathione defense fails in Parkinson's, into a short list of specific genes to follow up, and it separates the changes that are just neuron loss from the ones that point at regulation. The same approach extends to the lab's own models, comparing the substantia nigra signature against iPSC dopamine neurons under oxidative stress to find which defects the cell models reproduce and which need the tissue context.

What I would bring

My background is in computational neuroscience and expression analysis. I am comfortable pulling and normalizing transcriptomic data, running differential expression and pathway-level comparisons, and building small tools that make a result easy to read. I would be glad to connect the lab's oxidative-stress and glutathione work to public and in-house datasets, and to help prioritize targets for the bench.

Methods note

Data: GEO GSE7621 (Lesnick et al.), postmortem human substantia nigra, 9 controls and 16 Parkinson's cases, Affymetrix U133 Plus 2.0. For each gene the highest-expressed probe was used; change is the log2 ratio of mean Parkinson's to mean control intensity, with a Welch t-statistic across samples; genes with absolute t at or above 2.07 are marked significant. This is bulk tissue, so lower dopamine-marker expression largely reflects loss of dopamine neurons rather than per-cell regulation, and antioxidant changes are read in the surviving mixed tissue. These are exploratory associations meant to prioritize follow-up, not confirmed mechanisms.
